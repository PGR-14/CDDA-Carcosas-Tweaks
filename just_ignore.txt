 Monologue for Saint Louis is, on the surface, about a woman coming home after a long time on the road.
But this would miss quite a few details important to the depth of the story. Through a large list of
metaphors, allusions, & comparisons, Colleen McElroy reveals not only the feelings about coming back
home, but also the feelings about growing up & changes in hometowns.

 The main metaphor, & recurring item, in the story are arbors. They are interlocked w/ roads, & travel 
in general. Paragraph 1 tells us her early experience travelling locally, to the neighbors. Paragraph 2 
shows us how, upon the first time she left, there were arbors all around. But paragraph 3 marks a 
difference, how said Arbors are 'crumbling heap(s) of rotting black sticks' due to the trellising of 
earthworms. This represents a few things, firstly, her coming home. This is pretty cut & dry, how the 
Arbors have died, she's come home because of it. But going a little deeper, this also represents the 
increased danger of the road, no longer being a home to her, & the rot represents a sort of sickness 
towards it. Alternatively, this rot could be considered that of crime & loss of moral values. It also 
represents her finality in coming home. She's looked around the city, seen how it has changed, & 
decided she still loves Saint Louis.

 A smaller addition I'd like to talk about is are the allusions she makes. They aren't very important
to the plot, nor detail, but they're very much worth covering, & they add quite a bit to the story. 
The first is, fittingly, the very first line. 'Home again and the heart barely there'. This connects 
to the old addage 'Home is where the heart is', as well as serving as a good opening to advance the 
story from it. Here, the saying has changed. Instead of being a sort of comfort to uncomfortable 
places, it has now turned into a sort of mourning. Like coming across an old camp of your own making.
